ABSTRACT Mathematical expressions are hard to read, which makes it hard to digitize printed STEM
material. Conventional OCR methods do not work well on contents which comprise of text along with
mathematical expressions. This paper proposes an automated book scanning system comprising of Raspberry
Pi, page gripper and flipper set up and a smartphone camera for capturing the content. Once the content is
captured, a combination of text recognition, mathematical expression detection and recognition modules
work in synergy to provide the final digitized content. The mathematical expression detection module
consists of a customized RoIHeads version of Faster R-CNN. The proposed model performed with 95.71%
precision, 91.77% recall, 93.74% F1-score, and 87.42% mean IOU on the publicly available IBEM dataset.
Once the expressions are detected, they are recognised using the Vision Encoder-Decoder Model architecture
and the subsequent latex version of the mathematical expression is generated. This model is trained on the
Im2Latex dataset and provided a BLEU score of 86.44 % on the test set. We also demonstrated the utility of
the proposed method in academic STEM book scanning with accurate recovery of mathematical expressions.
Our system gives a cost effective solution which makes it possible to extract text and mathematical content
from printed books in a method that is scalable and accurate. This opens the door to large-scale digital
preservation and machine-readable academic archiving.

I. INTRODUCTION
The digital transformation of educational resources, partic-
ularly in STEM fields, has become an essential component
of modern academics and archive science. This transi-
tion attempts to improve accessibility while also enabling
machine-readability for massive amounts of scientific knowl-
edge. Accurately recognizing and extracting mathematical
equations from printed materials is a significant issue in
this pursuit. Unlike conventional text, mathematical formulas
have two-dimensional layouts, specialized notations, and are
frequently embedded inside surrounding prose - character-
istics that make traditional Optical Character Recognition
(OCR) systems ineffective [1], [2]. These systems often

A. NOVELTY AND CONTRIBUTIONS
This paper proposes a comprehensive solution to both
algorithmic and practical limits in mathematical expression
recognition and book digitization. Specifically, our contri-
bution focuses on the integration of automated scanning
hardware with deep learning–based math expression detec-
tion and recognition modules. The integrated system helps in
automating page turning, image capturing, and processing,
which eliminates the need for operator intervention and
allows for continuous operation to create a cohesive, end-
to-end academic book digitization system. From a model
innovation perspective, we customize an existing model
(Faster R-CNN Architecture) with optimized region proposal
networks and fine-tuned RoI heads along with a lightweight
offline processing interface. The important innovations are:
• Fully Automated Book Scanning System: A novel,
low-cost scanning machine is created with inexpensive
and modular components. The system automates page
turning, image capturing, and processing, which elimi-
nates the need for operator intervention and allows for
continuous operation.
• Custom Faster R-CNN Architecture: We provide a
tailored Faster R-CNN model with optimized region
proposal networks and fine-tuned RoI heads. This model
is specifically designed to detect printed mathematical
expressions from STEM pages with high accuracy and
few false positives.
• Offline Processing Interface: We provide a lightweight
offline web interface for usage in bandwidth-constrained
or disconnected contexts. It allows users to upload
and batch process scanned pages, extract mathematical
expressions, and save results locally, ensuring accessi-
bility and scalability without the need for cloud services.

II. RELATED WORK
Automated book scanning has made significant progress,
with systems aimed to reduce manual intervention, improve
accuracy, and maximize cost effectiveness. Elder and Wong
created a low-cost robotic book scanner that can turn pages
and produce high-quality digital documents while preserving
the original materials [10]. Sinmaz et al. conducted a
thorough investigation into the effect of book precondition-
ing on the success rates of automated page-turning [12].
Similarly, Watanabe et al. showed a contactless page-turning
mechanism that used air suction to efficiently handle pages
at high rates, demonstrating the potential for large-scale
digitization projects [13].
Ata et al. presented the Automated Linear Book-Scanning
System (ALBS), with a sophisticated combination of pneu-
matic and mechanical elements to successfully automate
the scanning process
[14]. Nawshin et al. established
a low-cost automated scanner combined with translation
capabilities, exhibiting practical utility in varied educational
and library environments [15]. Furthermore, commercial
systems from businesses such as Xerox (Mandel et al., Xerox
Corporation) and Google (Qumsiyeh, Google Inc.) used
vacuum-based page-turning mechanisms in conjunction with
high-resolution photography, meeting industry standards for
digitization accuracy and throughput [5], [16].
Effective mathematical expression digitization is essen-
tially dependent on the correct detection and isolation of
mathematical content inside textual materials. The IBEM
dataset, proposed by Anitei et al., has considerably supported
this work by offering a dataset for mathematical expression
indexing and searching, serving as a critical standard for
future research in mathematical expression detection [17].
In addition to dataset development, Phong et al. intro-
duced a detection methodology based on Fast Fourier
Transform (FFT) and Support Vector Machines (SVM),
which recognises displayed mathematical expressions within
printed texts, resulting in improved precision in expression
localization [18]. Furthermore, Ohyama et al. used a U-Net
architecture trained on a wide range of scientific articles,
displaying robust identification skills even in the presence of
different expression layouts and typefaces [19].
Yu et al. proposed direct extraction of mathematical
expressions from PDF documents, which used intrinsic PDF
metadata and greatly outperformed standard image-based

in recognition capabilities by offering massive, diverse, and
annotated data [22]. Concurrently, Zhang and Li’s novel
integration of CNNs, Transformers, and diffusion-based data
augmentation techniques significantly improved recognition
accuracy [23]. Similarly, Li et al. used a YOLOv5-based
model enhanced with spatial attention mechanisms and
Bidirectional LSTM networks to achieve robust and accurate
handwritten mathematical expression recognition [24].
Aggarwal et al. conducted a comprehensive survey
of structural analysis methodologies across printed and
handwritten mathematical expressions, highlighting critical
differences in spatial relationship parsing strategies and
guiding future research toward more accurate semantic
interpretations of complex mathematical structures [25].
Integrated hybrid systems represent the cutting edge of cur-
rent research, merging hardware automation with advanced
recognition models. Sable et al.’s Doc-Handler demonstrated
this integration by smoothly combining computer vision,
natural language processing, and robotic manipulation to
considerably streamline document scanning and translation
[26]. Lee et al. exhibited electroadhesive forces for page-
turning, highlighting unique mechanical methods combined
with advanced image processing and recognition algorithms
to improve the reliability and accuracy of automated book
scanning [11]. Overall, while significant progress has been
made, several problems remain such as mechanical depend-
ability, improved identification accuracy across multiple
mathematical notations, and building cost-effective auto-
mated book scanning system with enhanced capabilities.
Addressing these difficulties is crucial for moving forward
with high-precision mathematical expression extraction and
STEM instructional material digitization.

IV. PROPOSED AUTOMATED BOOK SCANNER SYSTEM
This section discusses about the key components in the
printed mathematical expression recognition system as well
as the low cost automated book scanner system.
A. SOFTWARE ARCHITECTURE: PRINTED MATHEMATICAL
EXPRESSION RECOGNITION (PMER) SYSTEM
Figure 3 shows the general architecture of the Printed Math-
ematical Expression Recognition (PMER) system, which
includes an end-to-end workflow for recognizing, isolating,
and transforming printed mathematical expressions into
LaTeX representations. The system blends hardware and
software components into a single framework. It consists
of four major stages: Text detection, expression detection,
extraction, and recognition. Each stage is meticulously
planned to handle input images—captured using the auto-
mated book scanner (online system) or uploaded via the
offline web interface—and transform mathematical content
into organized digital representations. This modular pipeline
enables the flexibility, scalability, and interoperability of
various STEM publications.
1) EXPRESSION DETECTION FRAMEWORK
The initial phase of the PMER workflow focuses on
detecting spatial regions in scanned texts that contain
mathematical expressions. Specifically, it employs a Faster
R-CNN model that combines a ResNet-50 backbone with a
Feature Pyramid Network (FPN), Region Proposal Network
(RPN) and RoI pooling operations. As part of pre-processing,
normalization and augmentation is employed as illustrated in
Figure 3. Following pre-processing, the image is fed into the
ResNet50+FPN module, which extracts hierarchical features
at various scales. The RPN then generates object sugges-
tions, which may include mathematical expressions. These
suggestions are refined using RoI pooling and identified
as expression or non-expression areas. The post-processing
step uses Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS), confidence
thresholding, and bounding box regression to finalize predic-
tions. This stage produces a picture with localized bounding
boxes around the detected expressions and their associated
confidence ratings.
2) TEXT DETECTION FRAMEWORK
The next operation in the scanning pipeline is to identify and
extract plain text information from scanned book pictures,
which will serve as the foundation for subsequent expression-
specific processing. To accomplish this, we use the Tesseract
OCR engine [33], which employs a robust sequence
modeling pipeline that includes CNNs and Bidirectional
Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) layers, followed by a
transcription mechanism based on Connectionist Temporal
Classification. Tesseract-based detection is used to isolate
non-mathematical elements like paragraphs, headers, labels,
and annotations. Although this technique is not suitable
for identifying complicated mathematical notation due to
its linear sequence prediction limitations, it is critical in
distinguishing general textual content from mathematical
zones. These non-mathematical portions are subsequently
removed from the downstream mathematical expression
recognition module, increasing precision and processing
efficiency.
3) EXPRESSION RECOGNITION FRAMEWORK
The next step is to recognize the detected expression images
and turn them into LaTeX syntax. This is accomplished
through the use of a Vision Encoder-Decoder Model archi-
tecture, which combines a transformer-based visual encoder
(e.g., DeiT or ViT) and a transformer decoder (e.g., TrOCR)
that has been pretrained on written mathematical information.
Each cropped image is separated into a sequence of patches,
which are subsequently flattened and embedded in feature
vectors.
The encoder learns the formula’s contextual visual struc-
ture, including positional relationships between symbols.
The decoder uses masked multi-head attention and auto-
regressive decoding to convert these visual embeddings to
sequential LaTeX tokens. The predicted LaTeX sequence
is then post-processed to remove padding, special tokens,
and formatting issues. This recognition configuration enables
the system to correctly comprehend a wide range of
mathematical notation styles, from algebraic equations to
matrix or summation formats, displaying both generalization
and structural sensitivity. The TrOCR model implementa-
tion in this work was adapted from Microsoft’s official
repository [34].

4) EXPRESSION EXTRACTION FRAMEWORK
The final step is to extract the recognized mathematical
expression accurately. At this point, each bounding box
detected during detection is used to crop the mathematical
region from the original image. A Python-based post-
processing pipeline examines the spatial context of each
discovered region and divides it into two structural types:
embedded expressions (found within text lines) and isolated
expressions (standalone equations). An example of this can
be seen in Figure 3. This differentiation helps the clarity
of downstream recognition tasks. The trimmed expressions
are saved as discrete high-resolution PNG files, enabling
modular inclusion into future workflows. While, no deep
learning model is used at this stage, the accuracy and logical
grouping attained here serve an important role in preserving
consistency throughout the identification process.
B. HARDWARE ARCHITECTURE
The automated book scanning system was built with a major
focus on being cost-effective, adaptable, and self-sufficient
so that printed text may be digitized. Figure 4 shows that
the scanner has a V-shaped wooden cradle, custom-made
motorized arms, suction and air-based flipping mechanisms,
smartphone-based imaging, and a micro-controller based
control panel system.
The cradle is set at an angle of around 50 degrees, which
is the best angle to support books of different sizes while
putting less stress on the spine of the book. The system was
set up such that the pages would stay in the same place and
the spacing would be the same when imaging and flipping.

As seen in Table 4, the detection models differ greatly in

The suggested automated book scanning system was tested

In continuous operation, the device completes a full cycle
of page grabbing, image capture from two smartphones,
page turning, and resetting in 10 to 12 seconds. This timing

images, allowing all downstream processes such as cropping,
dewarping, expression identification, and snippet extraction

3) COST AND MARKET COMPARISON
The proposed automated book scanner was designed with the
purpose of providing an economical yet functional alternative
to existing commercial technologies. Unlike industrial-grade
scanners, which frequently include proprietary software and
sophisticated mechanical setups, this prototype was created
with off-the-shelf components, bespoke 3D-printed parts,
and open-source software integration. The system, consisting
of MG996R servo motors, dual blower fans, PC fan with
relay, Arduino Mega, Raspberry Pi 4B (8GB), control
panel electronics, power supply (500W SMPS and buck
converters), and physical frame (wooden and 3D-printed
parts), was built for a budget of around (Indian Rupee) Rs.
18,000 to Rs. 20,000 (equivalent to $215-$240 USD). This
makes it ideal for educational institutions, research labs,
and libraries that require low-cost digitization infrastructure
while maintaining automation and precision. In compari-
son, the suggested system provides a balanced solution,
including automatic page flipping, synchronized image
collection, and deep-learning-based mathematical expression
recognition—all at a substantially reduced cost. Table 10

D. OFFLINE PROCESSING SYSTEM AND WEB INTERFACE
To expand the utility of the proposed PMER system
beyond real-time collection, an offline batch-processing web

mated book scanner prototype designed specifically for
this project, which streamlines image acquisition using

recording. This technology is combined with a powerful
software pipeline designed to detect, extract, and recognize
printed mathematical formulas, providing a realistic solution
to digitize STEM literature. Multiple detection models were
tested, and Faster R-CNN with ResNet-50 custom trained

providing high precision and recall rates for expression local-

more robust pre-processing and dataset adaptability when
moving from training to practical use. The real-world testing
of the system included the digitization of academic textbooks
with complicated mathematical notation. It remained oper-
ationally stable across hundreds of pages, completing each
full cycle (gripping, turning, capturing, and resetting) in 10-
12 seconds. This resulted in a throughput of around 300-400
pages per hour. An offline web interface was also created,
allowing users to upload pre-scanned pages and process them
in batches without relying on scanner hardware, broadening
the system’s reach to contexts lacking real-time automation
infrastructure.
In terms of pricing, the proposed system outperforms
cutting-edge commercial book scanners, which often cost
thousands of dollars and rely on proprietary software. In com-
parison, our prototype was created for about $215-$240 USD
utilizing off-the-shelf and open-source components, while
yet providing complete automation and integrated expression
detection, a substantial contribution for educational institu-
tions and researchers with limited budgets.

With reference to the actual configuration as depicted in
Figure 16, the following are the key stages in the hardware
operation.
1) Stage 1 – Page Gripper and Image Capture: The
servo-controlled gripper arm descends, isolating one
page. Once stabilized, the Raspberry Pi sends a
capture command to the mounted cellphones. High-
resolution page photos are captured and transmitted to
the Pi, then to a laptop for preprocessing (including
cropping, dewarping, deskewing, and noise reduction).
The improved photos are then fed into the PMER
pipeline, which detects and recognizes mathematical
expressions.
2) Stage 2 – Gripper Retraction and Flipper Activa-
tion: Once the photos have been acquired, the gripper
arm is retracted. The page flipper mechanism (C-arm)
is actuated, sweeping in an arc from Position 1 to
Position 2. A suction conduit attached to the arm draws
the page up with airflow from Blower Fan 1.
3) Stage 3 – Suction, Flicking, and Air Assist:As the
flipping arm approaches its final position, the PC fan
is engaged via a relay, flicking the edge of the page
for better separation. A second blower on the other
side sends air toward the page, completing the flipping
process while preventing multi-page rotations.
4) Stage 4 – Cycle Completion and Reset: The flipper
arm returns to Position 1, and the system prepares for
the next cycle. A main reset switch stops all operations
and clears saved motor positions.
